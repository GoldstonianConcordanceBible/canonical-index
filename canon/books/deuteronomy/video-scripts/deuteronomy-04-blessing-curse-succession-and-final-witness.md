## Long-Form Script
The final section of Deuteronomy sharpens covenant into decision. Blessing and curse are set before the people. This means covenant is not symbolic only. Obedience and rebellion carry real consequences in history. The land will not magically preserve the people if their hearts turn away.

Chapter 28 is especially powerful because it makes the stakes unmistakable. Blessing follows obedience. Curse follows rebellion. Deuteronomy does not hide the covenant cost of disloyalty. This is fire at the threshold.

Yet the book also offers hope. Restoration is still possible. The word is near. Life may still be chosen. Covenant renewal in Moab shows that God continues to address His people even in the face of their future failure.

Joshua is then commissioned, showing that covenant continuity does not end with Moses. Leadership passes, but the word remains. The law is written, entrusted, and set as witness. The Song of Moses becomes a poetic testimony that will stand against forgetfulness and rebellion.

Moses then blesses the tribes, ascends the mountain, sees the land, and dies. His death on the threshold gives Deuteronomy its unique weight. Moses brought the people this far, but the future belongs to the covenant word of God, not to one human leader alone.

Under the Mirror → Water → Fire Doctrine, this final section closes the Torah with force.

Mirror: it reveals that covenant decisions have real consequences, and that the people must choose life under God.  
Water: it cleanses despair by keeping restoration and nearness of the word in view.  
Fire: it refines through witness, warning, succession, and the death of Moses at the threshold.

This is why Deuteronomy 27 through 34 matters. It is the final covenant charge of the Torah. Here the people are warned, blessed, commissioned, and handed forward into the future.

## Closing Line
Deuteronomy ends by showing that leaders may die, but the word of God remains, and the people must choose life under covenant.
