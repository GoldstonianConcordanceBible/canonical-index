## Long-Form Script
Leviticus begins with a call from the tabernacle. That detail matters. In Exodus, God filled the tabernacle with glory. In Leviticus, He now speaks from within it. The question is no longer whether God is present. The question is how the people can approach that presence rightly. The answer begins with sacrifice.

The opening chapters establish the offerings. Burnt offerings signify total consecration. Grain offerings represent dedication and thanksgiving. Peace offerings express fellowship. Sin offerings deal with defilement and guilt. Guilt offerings address trespass and restitution. Together, these sacrifices teach that worship, atonement, gratitude, and reconciliation are not improvised. Approach to God is structured.

This sacrificial system is not meaningless ritual. It forms Israel's understanding of holiness. Blood, fire, grain, oil, confession, and priestly handling all communicate the same truth: God is near, but He is not casual. Sin has cost. Worship has order. Reconciliation requires more than intention.

The priesthood then becomes central. Aaron and his sons are consecrated because mediation cannot be self-appointed. Those who stand between holy presence and sinful people must themselves be sanctified. Garments, oil, sacrifice, waiting, and public ordination all underscore the seriousness of priestly service.

When priestly ministry begins, the glory of God appears. This is a moment of confirmation. The sacrificial order is not empty form. Divine fire answers. Worship is accepted. The people witness that ordered holiness opens real access to God.

But then comes one of the most sobering moments in the Torah: Nadab and Abihu offer unauthorized fire before the Lord. Their act is judged immediately. Leviticus makes the point without confusion: holy worship cannot be redefined on human terms. Creativity that dishonors holiness is not faithfulness. Nearness to God increases responsibility.

This section therefore establishes the foundations of approach: sacrifice, mediation, consecration, and reverence. It teaches that redeemed people still need priestly order if they are to live before divine holiness.

Under the Mirror → Water → Fire Doctrine, this section becomes unmistakable.

Mirror: it reveals that access to God requires sacrifice and authorized mediation.  
Water: it cleanses the idea that worship can be self-designed without consequence.  
Fire: it refines through altar, priesthood, and the judgment of unauthorized approach.

This is why Leviticus 1 through 10 matters. It lays the foundation for the whole book by showing that holiness is not abstract. It governs how people draw near, how priests serve, and how worship is offered before the living God.

## Closing Line
Leviticus begins by teaching that if God is truly present, then worship must be holy, ordered, and offered on His terms.
